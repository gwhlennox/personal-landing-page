When I joined SideShift.ai in April 2025, there was no formal partnership function. No process, no CRM, no playbook. Just a list of integrations that had happened organically over time and a product that was quietly becoming infrastructure for a growing slice of the Web3 ecosystem.

My job was to build something that worked — and to do it as a team of one.

## Starting from zero

The first thing I did was map what already existed. Which wallets were integrated? Which exchanges listed our token? Where were we showing up in the ecosystem without anyone actively managing the relationship?

The picture was actually more encouraging than I expected. SideShift had meaningful traction with a handful of well-respected wallets — but the relationships were dormant. Nobody was talking to these partners. Nobody was asking what they needed, or thinking about how to deepen the integration.

That became the first priority: activate what we already had.

## Building the motion

I spent the first 60 days meeting partners. Not to pitch, but to listen. What were they building? Where did they see friction? What would a better version of our relationship look like for them?

From those conversations, a pattern emerged. Partners wanted three things: faster technical support, visibility into our roadmap, and some form of co-marketing. None of these were complicated. They just required consistency.

So I built a simple operating rhythm. Monthly check-ins with top-tier partners. A shared Notion space with product updates. A light co-marketing programme where we'd amplify each other's announcements.

Within a quarter, the relationships felt different. Partners were reaching out proactively. Conversations that had been cold were now warm.

## The hackathon

The project I'm most proud of from this period was the Akindo Buildathon — a global hackathon we designed and ran in collaboration with the Akindo platform.

We attracted 164 builders across 13 countries, received 68 submissions, and got one project all the way to a live launch. For a team of 18 people with no events budget or prior event experience, that felt like a meaningful proof of concept for what the SideShift ecosystem could become.

## What I'd do differently

If I were starting again, I'd invest earlier in data. I spent too long tracking partnership activity in spreadsheets before moving to a proper lightweight CRM. The cost of that delay showed up in dropped follow-ups and missed context when conversations resumed after a gap.

I'd also have pushed harder for clearer internal alignment on what success looked like. Partnerships can mean many things — distribution, credibility, revenue, product feedback. Without a shared definition, it's easy to optimise for the wrong thing.
